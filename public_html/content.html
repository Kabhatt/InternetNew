The real process and the accelerated process of creating the internt started in the last century, during the Cold-War. The then US president, President Eisenhower, understood the great need of attaining a better and more efficient communication method across the country, after seeing the devastation Nuclear bombs in Japan caused. It is important to remember that back in the cold war era, the world's communication system worked on thses call centers relied on phone operators to make the connections between telephone lines manually. This was a huge issue of national concern because if USSR droped a nuclear bomb or a war emerged and one of these telephone service centers were affected that would have caused a complete abrution and delay in communicating important messages for National security, this concern was amplified with the launch of Sputnick in 1958. So in a move to build a better National Secrutity system, in 1958, the US government forms ARPA (eventually became DARPA) or the Advanced Research Projects Agency. ARPA used to focus on space and technology, but Nasa eventually was created in 1962 and thus space became NASA’s jurisdiction and United Stated started to focus on space travel.• It wasn't until 1962, when an MIT psychologist by the name of J.C.R Liklieder, came up with a basic idea of the internet by theorizing a way to connect networks for computers that accelerated the process further. This gave the developers a structure of what the internet should be like. The in 1962 Leonard Kleinrock wrote a thesis in Queuing Theory, in which, a message would arrive and hang around for a while and leave. Then in 1963 ASCII are developed, these were the American standard code for information exchange. This became the bases of what information exchange would need to meet the requirement of getting messages through.Things were still set in th early phases of getting the machinery that we call the internet started. It wasn't until • Paul Barran came up with hot potato routing, where the data of the phone calls would be chopped up to smaller pieces and they would travel towards nodes and they would be recorded and kept safe, even if some area of the network was destroyed these nodes would still have back up of that same data. This method was inspired by a mouse in a maze study. This was a revolutionary idea in data communications. Barran used the idea from telegram to chop up the messages in to packages and around this time Bob Taylor asked ARPA for funding to find a way to connect heterogenous computers through a network. He was granted $1 million for his idea and research. • By the end of 1969 all the 4 mainframe computers in various locations in California were interconnected all by ARPANet. Larry Roberts was recruited by Bob Taylor, who already built the first network at MIT Lincoln laboratories between two computers. Then in 1965 Intel co-founder Gordon Moore discovered the Moore’s law, in which he states that the number of transistors that fit in to chip doubles once every two years. Allowing for computers to become smaller in size. This was an important feature for silicon valley’s entrepreneurs in the 90s.

While the smaller sizes of computers were preparing to give rise to many future billionaires and millionaires of Silicone Valley, Larry Roberts was recruiting engineers to build the ARPANet which would eventually become the internet, in 1968. This It was based on interface message processors or IMPs, and based on packet switching. This experiment was done on 4 different mainframe computers, all located in various Californian universities, but done with IMPs. In 1969, BBN took the project of packet switching communication systems, and started to build the IMPs for the ARPAnet. Dr. Stephen Crocker, a graduate student, wrote the code for the first IMPs for ARPAnet and in 1969 the first piece of hardware created for the first piece of the forerunner of the internet arrived to UCLA. This eventually became Metcalf’s law, and influenced silicon valley’s production in the 1990s. In 1983 TCPIP was adopted as the universal standard of internet network.
